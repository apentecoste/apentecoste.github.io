Visualizing the Refugee Crisis

By Vera Tang, Kartikeya Lakhanpal, Paul-Emile Gras and Arthur Pentecoste

CDGV Hackathon

The world has witnessed an unprecedented migration crisis in the past few years. With over 40 Million people displaced and coerced to migrate the around the world, we look at the data available to visualize patterns that Refugees and displaced people exhibit in order to understand the urgency of the situation better and possible explore solutions to the refugee crisis.

Overview of the refugee crisis

Data Source: [UNHCR 2018]

First let’s understand the global nature of the problem and which countries have become the hotspots for migration. You can click on a country to see the most common pattern of movement the people leaving that country exhibit.

A key factor in migration is the distance that people must travel. Below, we have visualized the initial tendency to migrate to the neighboring countries first. We mapped the net difference between people entering a country and leaving the country and you can observe formations of hotspots where a country that has more people leaving is surrounded by countries that had more people entering it in the same year.

Countries of Origin of the refugees

After Identifying countries that have highest outflux of refugees, we try visualizing the reasons for their possible migration. Interestingly, we see that a. countries in green are countries which have reported persecution against ethnic/religious minorities and therefore have higher migration but report fewer violent incidents b. Yemen/Ukraine are countries undergoing civil disruptions and thus have reported much higher incidents of violence and yet have not had as many people leave the country. c. Afghanistan is a country marred by terrorism and shows an intuitive correlation between more refugees and more incidents of violence.

Countries of Asylum

These geopolitical differences in the countries of origin explain differences in the countries of asylum. Usually, it’s a country with similar cultural ties and geographically closer. We notice significant movements of people from Syria to Turkey and between Afghanistan and Pakistan.

Consequences for Host countries

Data Source: [UNHCR 2018][World Happiness Report 2019][IMF World Data 2018] [Freedom of Speech Index 2019]

Now we visualize general trends in countries that have been the top acceptors for refugees. Please note that there is no strict implication that migration has a causal effect on the indicators visualized. Contrary to popular belief, we note no significant change in the happiness Index, Unemployment rate or Inflation of prices for goods&services for the countries that accepted refugees.

To conclude, the visualizations are an attempt to observe migration trends among refugees and we consider certain indicators to observe the impact of migration on countries.
